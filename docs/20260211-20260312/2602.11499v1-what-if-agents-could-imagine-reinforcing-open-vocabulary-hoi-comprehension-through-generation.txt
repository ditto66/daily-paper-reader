## Abstract 

Multimodal Large Language Models have shown promising capabilities in bridging visual and tex-tual reasoning, yet their reasoning capabilities in Open-Vocabulary Human-Object Interaction (OV-HOI) are limited by cross-modal hallucina-tions and occlusion-induced ambiguity. To ad-dress this, we propose ImagineAgent , an agen-tic framework that harmonizes cognitive reason-ing with generative imagination for robust visual understanding. Specifically, our method innova-tively constructs cognitive maps that explicitly model plausible relationships between detected entities and candidate actions. Subsequently, it dynamically invokes tools including retrieval aug-mentation, image cropping, and diffusion models to gather domain-specific knowledge and enriched visual evidence, thereby achieving cross-modal alignment in ambiguous scenarios. Moreover, we propose a composite reward that balances predic-tion accuracy and tool efficiency. ## 1. Introduction 

Human-Object Interaction (HOI) detection (Gupta & Malik, 2015) aims to identify and localize interacting human and object pairs within an image, while also recognizing their semantic relationships as <human, object, action> triplets. This fine-grained understanding of human-centric activities is foundational for next-generation applications in areas such as robot manipulation, assistive technologies, and intel-ligent surveillance (Liu et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024). To truly unlock this potential, a critical frontier is extending HOI detection beyond a predefined, closed set of categories to an open-vocabulary (OV) setting (Wang et al., 2022; Lei et al., 2024b; Hu et al., 2025). Recent breakthroughs in Multimodal Large Language Mod-els (MLLMs) (Radford et al., 2021; Hurst et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Comanici et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025) have revolutionized both visual perception and reasoning. By leveraging the vast world knowledge embedded in their underlying language models and grounding it in visual ev-idence, MLLMs can interpret nuanced visual details and their semantic implications with high fidelity. Perceptual-Cognitive Gap : As illustrated in Fig. 1 (b), while MLLMs detect all persons and objects individually, they often fail to establish semantically coherent physical relationships between them. This stems from a lack of struc-tured cognitive mapping that connects visual perception to plausible interaction hypotheses, which may cause incom-plete or fragmented reasoning despite accurate detection. 

❷ Cross-Modal Hallucination : As shown in Fig. 1 (c), MLLMs frequently generate plausible-sounding but visu-ally unsupported interactions, driven by textual priors rather than visual cues. This cross-modal hallucination arises from imprecise alignment between high-level semantic concepts and low-level visual features, especially under distribution shifts in open-vocabulary settings. ❸ Occlusion-induced Ambiguity : As depicted in Fig. 1 (d), real-world scenes frequently encounter partial occlusions or suboptimal view-points, which truncate critical visual features for interaction verification. Without mechanisms to recover limited visual information, MLLMs may resort to biased textual priors, causing incorrect predictions in ambiguous scenarios. To address above-mentioned challenges, we argue that an effective OV-HOI agent requires three synergistic capabil-ities: ❶ Cognitive Mapping , which adopts a structured reasoning framework that explicitly models the cognitive mapping between detected entities and possible actions. By bridging the gap from perception to cognition, this structure enables the model to construct comprehensive interaction hypotheses with physical plausibility. ❷ Tool Augmenting .The agent learns to dynamically invoke domain-specific tools for gathering targeted visual-semantic evidence dur-ing reasoning. Such an interactive workflow transforms the static foundation model into an interactive agent, effectively reinforcing cross-modal alignment while suppressing hallu-cinations. ❸ Generative World Modeling , which adopts diffusion-based autoregressive models to reconstruct plausi-ble alternative views under occlusion, effectively expanding its observational horizon. This generative prior acts as a world model that empowers the agent to "imagine" through ambiguity, moving beyond derivation from known informa-tion. It is precisely this principle of augmenting perception with imagination that motivates our framework. Therefore, we introduce ImagineAgent , a novel framework that integrates cognitive reasoning, generative imagination, and tool-augmented reinforcement learning (RL) for robust open-vocabulary HOI detection. Specifically, our method first establishes a cognitive map by structuring perceived entities into comprehensive interaction hypotheses, directly bridging the perception-to-cognition gap. Subsequently, the interactive agent learns to dynamically invoke multimodal tools to enhance both physical reasoning and spatial ground-ing capabilities. To mitigate cross-modal hallucinations, it employs both retrieval augmentation and image cropping to gather domain-specific knowledge and targeted visual evi-dence. To resolve occlusion-induced ambiguity, it utilizes a generative world model to imagine and reconstruct alter-native viewpoints. This entire workflow is guided by RL to optimize both the precision & recall of HOI predictions and the efficiency of tool utilization. Our contributions are: • Tool-Augmented Agentic RL. ImagineAgent introduces a novel framework that unifies both cognitive mapping rea-soning and generative modeling with tool-augmentation reinforcement learning, thereby enabling robust OV-HOI through dynamic, visually grounded reasoning. • Generative World Modeling. We pioneer the integration of generative imagination as a world model. This allows the agent to "imagine" alternative viewpoints or complete occluded scenes, thereby transforming the agent from a passive observer into an active reasoner. • Empirical Effectiveness. ## 2. Methodology 

Overview. We propose ImagineAgent, a novel framework that integrates cognitive reasoning with generative imagina-tion and tool-augmented RL for robust OV-HOI, as shown in Fig. 2. Section 2.1 presents the problem formulation and our workflow. Section 2.2 details the Training Data Construction , which synthesizes structured reasoning chain essential for tool invocation and interaction detection. Sec-tion 2.3 describes the Agentic Supervised Fine-tuning ,where the Qwen2.5-VL base model is pretrained to initialize the agent’s policy for tool invocation. Section 2.4 outlines the Agentic Reinforcement Learning , which employs the GRPO algorithm to optimize agent policy via a composite reward that balances prediction accuracy and tool efficiency. 

2.1. Problem Formulation & Workflow Design 

The goal in OV-HOI is to identify and classify comprehen-sive interactions from a vocabulary unseen during training. The input is a single image I and a query Q, while the output is a set of interaction triplets H = {⟨ hk, o k, v k⟩} Kk=1 , where 

hk is a human, ok is an object, and vk is their interaction verb. We deconstruct the problem into a two-stage process: • Perception. This stage involves identifying and localizing all potential human and object instances within the image 

I to produce sets of localized human instances IH =

{(hi, b i)}Ni=1 and object instances IO = {(oj , b j )}Mj=1 ,where b denote the entity’s bounding box. • Cognition : This stage involves reasoning over the per-ceived instances to correctly pair entities and identify their interaction. It pairs instances (hi, o j ) from IH × I O and predicts all plausible interaction verb vk, thereby forming each valuable triplet ⟨hi, o j , v k⟩ ∈ H as final prediction. To achieve this goal, we design following two-stage work-flow that dynamically integrates domain-specific tools:

2.2. Training Data Construction 

A fundamental challenge when applying existing MLLMs to OV-HOI tasks is the absence of explicit reasoning chains. Standard pipelines provide static labels but lack intermediate cognitive steps: what tool to use, which human-object pairs to link, and how plausible verbs are identified. To address this, we devise a process to synthesize a high-fidelity structured reasoning chains, thus creating explicit examples that effectively bridge the perception-to-cognition gap. 

Data Collection. Our data generation process begins by leveraging the Qwen2.5-VL-72B (Bai et al., 2025a) model to explore the HICO-DET training dataset. For each im-age, we perform 16 independent policy rollouts to generate a diverse set of potential reasoning pathways. Instances that demonstrate at least one successful prediction across these rollouts are identified as solvable and selected for CoT generation. 

Data Assessment. To ensure the quality and reliability of the synthesized data, we introduce a rigorous assessment stage. Specifically, we employ the Qwen-VL-Max model as an expert validator to meticulously review and score each reasoning chain. Chains containing factual errors, logical inconsistencies, or hallucinated reasoning are systematically discarded. This validation procedure yields approximately 6,000 high-fidelity reasoning chains for SFT. The remaining 8,000 trajectories, which were confirmed as successful but not selected in SFT, are then served as the training data for RL stage, providing a wider range of successful behaviors for policy exploration. Moreover, during the selection of both SFT and RL, we further ensure the dataset maintains a high degree of diversity across different interaction types. 

Generating Pipeline. Our generating pipeline implements a three-step reasoning process. First, based on the initial perception stage, the agent identifies all human instances 

IH and object instances IO . Second, it constructs a com-prehensive hypothesis space by performing an exhaustive pairing of all perceived entities, creating a set of candidate human-object pairs (hi, o j ) ∈ I H × I O . Finally, for each pair, the agent evaluates a constrained set of valid verbs 

vk, scoring each of them based on visual evidence and 4contextual cues. Actions with high scores deemed valid and selected to constitute all validated interaction triplets 

H = {⟨ hk, o k, v k⟩} Kk=1 . This structured approach ensures comprehensive consideration of all potential HOIs, effec-tively bridging the perception-to-cognition gap.

However, preliminary experiments reveal a progressive decline in the frequency of tool invocation during policy rollouts. ## 3. Experiment 

Datasets . We perform our experiments on two datasets: HICO-DET (Chao et al., 2018) and SWIG-HOI (Wang et al., 2021). HICO-DET includes 600 interaction types made from 117 human actions and 80 objects. Following previous studies (Hou et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022), we exclude 120 rare interactions to test our model in a zero-shot detection setting. SWIG-HOI provides a wide range of interactions with 400 actions and 1,000 objects. This test set naturally contains many unseen combinations. It has about 14K images and 5.5K interaction types, including 1.8K interactions that are not present during training. 

Evaluation Metrics . Following prior work (Wang et al., 2022; Lei et al., 2024b; 2025), we evaluate our model using mean Average Precision (mAP). A prediction is a true posi-tive if it satisfies: (1) the Intersection over Union (IoU) for both the human and object bounding boxes is greater than 0.5. (2) the predicted interaction label is correct. 

Implementation Details . We perform experiments with Qwen2.5-VL-7B as our base model. According to Sec. 2.2, we respectively select approximately 6,000 and 8,000 train-ing samples for both SFT and RL. The training was con-ducted on 8 NVIDIA H20 GPUs (96 GB VRAM) with batch size of 8. The training process took around 40 hours for 1,000 iterations (1 epoch), with 4 rollouts per sample and a learning rate of 5e-7. More details are in Appendix A.1.2.

## 4. Related Work (Extended Ver. in Appx. C) 

Open-Vocabulary HOI Detection. Previous methods for open-vocabulary Human-Object Interaction (HOI) detection primarily leverage the cross-modal alignment capability of CLIP (Liao et al., 2022; Ning et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023), and utilize DETR (Carion et al., 2020) as a critical com-ponent to perform object detection in the first place (Kim et al., 2025). With the advances of Large Language Mod-els (LLMs), recent literature (Lei et al., 2024a;b; 2025) proposed to improve the fine-grained understanding of di-verse HOI concepts through prompting LLMs. Furthermore, MLLMs (Bai et al., 2025a) show great potential in open-vocabulary HOI detection in an end-to-end manner with strong visual grounding ability and world knowledge. Thus, we build ImagineAgent upon MLLMs and further achieve tool-augmented RL for robust open-vocabulary inference. 

Multimodal LLMs Reasoning. Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated that RL-based post-training can significantly enhance reasoning capabil-ities, as exemplified by OpenAI-o1 (Jaech et al., 2024) and DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025). These paradigms have been extended to Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) for tasks like mathematical VQA (Peng et al., 2025), image segmentation (Liu et al., 2025), and video understanding (Feng et al., 2025). However, existing meth-ods struggle with long-sequence hallucination (Chen et al., 2025b) and limited cross-modal interaction. To address this, we propose multimodal CoT reasoning via tool-augmented RL to explicitly reduce cross-modal hallucination. 

Tool-Augmented Agentic System. Recent advancements in MLLMs have shown that external tools can enhance mul-timodal reasoning. Early works like FAST (Sun et al., 2025) and MVoT (Li et al., 2025) introduce visual evidence into reasoning, forming multimodal CoT for image tasks. LLaVa-Plus (Liu et al., 2023) pioneered training strategies for tool use, while VPD (Hu et al., 2024) leveraged program-derived data to transfer tool skills. Recent methods like TACO (Ma et al., 2024) and PyVision (Zhao et al., 2025) expand tool use with RL. However, existing approaches rely on static tool pipelines, limiting their ability to complex, open-vocabulary settings. Our framework addresses this by unifying both cognitive mapping reasoning and generative modeling with tool-augmented RL for robust OV-HOI prediction. 

## 5. Conclusion 

In this work, we introduced ImagineAgent, a novel agentic framework that harmonizes cognitive reasoning with gen-erative imagination and tool-augmented RL for OV-HOI tasks. By constructing cognitive maps and dynamically in-voking multimodal tools, our approach effectively address the critical challenges of perceptual-cognitive gap, cross-8modal hallucination, and occlusion-induced ambiguity. The integration of a composite reward function within GRPO algorithm optimizes for both prediction accuracy and tool-usage efficiency. Extensive evaluations on the SWIG-HOI and HICO-DET datasets validate our SOTA performance, requiring only 20% of training data compared to others. Fu-ture work will focus on expanding the agent’s tool library and applying this imagination-driven reasoning paradigm to a broader range of complex visual comprehension tasks.

## A. Experiment 

A.1. Full Experimental Setup (Reproducibility) 

A.1.1. D ATASETS AND EVALUATION PROTOCOLS 

HICO-DET We conduct experiments on HICO-DET, a standard benchmark for HOI detection with 600 interaction categories composed of 117 verbs and 80 object classes and use the official test set for evaluation. Following prior zero-shot HOI protocols (CMD-SE (Lei et al., 2024b) and INP-CC (Lei et al., 2025)), 120 rare interaction categories are excluded from training to simulate the zero-shot setting, where these categories are treated as Unseen, and the remaining categories are treated as Seen. Results on Unseen, Seen, and Full splits are reported. 

SWIG-HOI We also evaluate on SWIG-HOI, a large-vocabulary HOI benchmark built upon the publicly available SWiG dataset to study large-vocabulary and open-vocabulary HOI detection. SWIG-HOI contains more than 400 action (verb) categories and 1,000 object categories, substantially expanding the label space compared to earlier datasets such as HICO-DET. In our setting, Unseen refers to out-of-vocabulary action types that do not appear in the training set, and we report results on the standard Non-rare, Rare, Unseen, and Full splits. 

Metric. We follow prior work and evaluate using mean Average Precision (mAP) computed by the official evaluation scripts, with no additional filtering. 

True positive criterion. A predicted HOI triplet is considered correct if it satisfies both: (i) IoU > 0.5 for the predicted human bounding box and its matched ground-truth human box, and IoU > 0.5 for the predicted object bounding box and its matched ground-truth object box. (ii) the predicted interaction label matches the ground truth. A.1.2. I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS AND HYPERPARAMETERS 

Base Model. We choose Qwen2.5-VL-7B as our backbone and fine-tune the model using full-parameter finetuning. 

Input Configuration. For each sample, the maximum image resolution is set to 401408 pixels, and the number of image tokens is capped at 512. The maximum text sequence length is 8192 tokens, and the maximum generation length during rollouts is 4096 tokens. We use resize and center crop for image preprocessing. 

Optimization. We train the model with Adam optimizer using a learning rate of 1e-6 for SFT Stage, and 5e-7 for RL Stage with weight decay 0.01, and gradient clipping with max norm 5. All experiments are conducted with BF16 mixed precision, and the random seed is fixed to 1234 for reproducibility. 

Batching and Training Budget. We use a per-device batch size of 1 on 8 GPUs, with gradient accumulation steps 2 for SFT and 1 for RL. The resulting global batch size is therefore 16 = 1 × 8 × 2 for SFT, and 8 = 1 × 8 × 1 for RL. 

SFT Stage. In supervised fine-tuning (SFT), we train for 1 epoch and use 5999 trajectories. 

RL Stage (GRPO). In agentic reinforcement learning, we optimize the policy using GRPO for 914 steps. For each training query, we sample 4 rollouts. We use KL regularization coefficient β = 0 .04 . The reference policy πref is chosen as SFT model, and the old policy πθold is updated every 1 steps. 

Hardware. Training is performed on eight Nvidia H20 GPUs with 96GB memory for each GPU. The total training time is approximately 2.5 hours for SFT and 40 hours for RL. 

A.2. Data Construction Details (Reasoning Chains) 

This section details the reproducible pipeline used to synthesize structured reasoning chains for HOI comprehension on HICO-DET. Our construction is implemented as a two-turn agentic prompting process with optional auxiliary images and optional textual knowledge, followed by trajectory packaging into training-ready JSON. 12 A.2.1. G ENERATION PROMPTS 

Prompt design (two-turn decomposition). We generate reasoning chains using a two-turn chat template with Qwen2.5-VL-72B-Instruct (served by vLLM). Turn-1 performs object set selection and tool selection , and Turn-2 performs exhaustive HOI enumeration under strict constraints and outputs HOI triplets with bounding boxes. 

Turn-1 (Perception & Tool Decision). Given the input image, the model is instructed to: (i) detect all visible objects from the predefined HICO object list. (ii) decide which tools to use among { image_description , action_description ,

outpaint , viewpoint_transform , image_crop }. The output format is enforced as: object_1, [ox1,oy1,ox2,oy2], object_2, [ox1,oy1,ox2,oy2], ... ; tool_1, tool_2, ... wrapped by <think> and <answer> tags. For training-data synthesis only, we additionally provide ground-truth objects extracted from the HOI annotation string inside the prompt to bias the chain toward GT-aligned decisions (used for CoT supervision rather than evaluation). 

Turn-2 (Constrained HOI Chain-of-Thought). Turn-2 takes the original image (always) plus up to two optional auxiliary images (outpaint and/or viewpoint transform) depending on Turn-1 tool decisions. It also injects: (i) DETECTED OBJECTS from Turn-1. (ii) a per-object VALID ACTIONS constraint list derived from HICO interactions (i.e., allowed verbs for each object class). (iii) optional textual evidence, including an image-level description and/or action definitions if the corresponding tools were selected. Inside <think> , the model must follow a fixed structure: (1) entity listing with person/object boxes, (2) exhaustive person– object pairing, (3) per-pair verb feasibility checks restricted to valid verbs, (4) final review and compilation. Inside <answer> ,the model must emit HOIs as a comma-separated list of records: idx: action, object, [px1,py1,px2,py2], [ox1,oy1,ox2,oy2] .For synthesis, we include the ground-truth HOI string in the prompt as a reference target (the model is instructed to build a reasoning bridge that leads to the GT). 

Tool library and how it is realized in our code. Unlike an online tool-execution agent, our synthesis pipeline implements tool augmentation by conditional evidence injection :• image_description : if selected in Turn-1, we load a precomputed image-level analysis from train_all_description.json 

and append it into the Turn-2 prompt. • action_description : if selected, we load per-action textual definitions from hico_actions_rephase_20.json . To avoid leaking irrelevant knowledge, we only include definitions for actions appearing in the valid-action constraint set of the current sample. • outpaint / viewpoint_transform : We explicitly instruct the model that auxiliary images are only for disambiguating actions, and that all bounding box coordinates must be taken from the original image. • image_crop : if selected, we extract high-resolution sub-regions centered on specific human-object pairs from the original image and inject them as auxiliary visual evidence into the Turn-2 prompt. 

Independent rollouts and decoding. In the released synthesis code, we generate one trajectory per image-query pair with stochastic decoding: temperature 0.8, and max generation length 4096 tokens for both turns. 

Randomness control. The synthesis code relies on the underlying sampler randomness from vLLM at the process level and does not explicitly set per-sample random seeds. ## B. Tool Library Details 

This section provides a detailed specification of the multimodal tools integrated into our ImagineAgent framework. These tools are designed to resolve specific ambiguities encountered during HOI comprehension and are categorized into visual refinement, generative imagination, and online retrieval-augmentation tools. 

B.1. Visual Refinement Tools 

To ensure that fine-grained interaction details are not overlooked in complex scenes, our framework incorporates an Image Crop tool ( Tc). Its primary function is to allow the agent to isolate and scrutinize high-resolution sub-regions of the input image. This tool is dynamically invoked when the initial perception suggests a potential interaction, but the visual evidence is too small or subtle to be conclusively identified from a global view. By extracting a focused patch centered on a candidate human-object pair, the agent can perform a more detailed analysis of subtle cues, such as hand-object contact or the specific orientation of a tool. This capability is critical for accurately distinguishing between similar interactions and directly mitigates failures arising from the perceptual-cognitive gap, where global context is insufficient for precise grounding. 

B.2. Online Retrieval-Augmentation Tools 

To mitigate cross-modal hallucinations, we leverage the Qwen API as an online Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) tool. This component anchors the agent’s reasoning in external, factual knowledge, preventing over-reliance on learned textual priors. It serves two key functions: Action Explanation (Ta) and Scene Explanation (Ts). When faced with semantically similar verbs, the agent can invoke Action Explanation to retrieve precise definitions and distinguishing characteristics, thus enabling a more nuanced classification. Concurrently, the Scene Explanation function provides high-level contextual descriptions of the entire image, which helps ground the agent’s understanding of the overall environment and the plausibility of certain interactions within it. By dynamically injecting this external knowledge, the framework ensures that predictions are semantically consistent with both the visual evidence and real-world knowledge. 

B.3. Generative Imagination Tools 

To address challenges of occlusion-induced ambiguity, our agent is endowed with generative imagination capabilities through the BAGEL model (Deng et al., 2025). This toolset operates in two distinct modes: Outpaint (To) and Viewpoint Transform (Tv ). The Outpaint function is employed when key contextual elements are cut off by the image frame, allowing the agent to reconstruct a more complete scene and better infer the overall activity. The Viewpoint Transform function is selected when an object or part of an interaction is occluded from the current perspective. By synthesizing a plausible alternative view, the agent can "see" behind obstructions, effectively filling in missing visual information. This process provides the agent with an imagined reality upon which to base its reasoning, transforming an incomplete observation into a more comprehensive one and enabling robust decision-making under visual uncertainty. Moreover, to control for potential generative artifacts and ensure the reliability of the agent’s reasoning, we embed a strict protocol within its prompt, governing the use of supplementary images. The complete instructions are detailed in Fig. 8. 2.3. SUPPLEMENTARY IMAGE DESCRIPTIONS

15 C. Related Work 

Open-Vocabulary HOI Detection. Previous methods for open-vocabulary Human-Object Interaction (HOI) detection primarily leverage the cross-modal alignment capability of CLIP (Liao et al., 2022; Ning et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023), and utilize DETR (Carion et al., 2020) as a critical component to perform object detection in the first place (Kim et al., 2025). With the advances of Large Language Models (LLMs), recent literature (Lei et al., 2024a;b; 2025) proposed to improve the fine-grained understanding of diverse HOI concepts through prompting LLMs. Furthermore, MLLMs (Bai et al., 2025a) show great potential in open-vocabulary HOI detection in an end-to-end manner with strong visual grounding ability and world knowledge. UniVG-R1 (Bai et al., 2025b) further demonstrates that reasoning-guided visual grounding with reinforcement learning can improve spatial localization under open-vocabulary settings. Complementing this direction, Video-STAR (Yuan et al., 2025b) reinforces open-vocabulary action recognition via tool-augmented reasoning, highlighting the importance of external tools for disambiguating fine-grained interactions. Thus, we build ImagineAgent upon MLLMs and further achieve tool-augmented RL for robust open-vocabulary inference. 

Multimodal LLMs Reasoning. Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated that RL-based post-training can significantly enhance reasoning capabilities, as exemplified by OpenAI-o1 (Jaech et al., 2024) and DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025). GPG (Chu et al., 2025) establishes a simple yet strong RL baseline for model reasoning, providing practical insights for reward design in complex reasoning tasks. These paradigms have been extended to Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) for tasks like mathematical VQA (Peng et al., 2025), image segmentation (Liu et al., 2025), and video understanding (Feng et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025a). To improve reasoning efficiency, HS-STAR (Xiong et al., 2025) introduces hierarchical sampling with difficulty-aware budget reallocation for self-taught reasoners. However, existing methods struggle with long-sequence hallucination (Chen et al., 2025b) and limited cross-modal interaction. To address this, we propose multimodal CoT reasoning via tool-augmented RL to explicitly reduce cross-modal hallucination. 

Tool-Augmented Agentic System. Recent advancements in MLLMs have shown that external tools can enhance multimodal reasoning. Early works like FAST (Sun et al., 2025) and MVoT (Li et al., 2025) introduce visual evidence into reasoning, forming multimodal CoT for image tasks. LLaVa-Plus (Liu et al., 2023) pioneered training strategies for tool use, while VPD (Hu et al., 2024) leveraged program-derived data to transfer tool skills. Recent methods like TACO (Ma et al., 2024) and PyVision (Zhao et al., 2025) expand tool use with RL. Notably, AutoDrive-R2 (Yuan et al., 2025a) incentivizes reasoning and self-reflection capacity for VLA models through tool-augmented RL in dynamic environments. However, existing approaches rely on static tool pipelines, limiting their ability to complex, open-vocabulary settings. Our framework addresses this by unifying both cognitive mapping reasoning and generative modeling with tool-augmented RL for robust prediction.
