Abstract 

Recent advances in generative AI have signifi-cantly enhanced the realism of multimodal me-dia manipulation, thereby posing substantial challenges to manipulation detection. Exist-ing manipulation detection and grounding ap-proaches predominantly focus on manipulation type classification under result-oriented super-vision, which not only lacks interpretability but also tends to overfit superficial artifacts. In this paper, we argue that generalizable detection requires incorporating explicit forensic reason-ing, rather than merely classifying a limited set of manipulation types, which fails to gen-eralize to unseen manipulation patterns. To this end, we propose REFORM , a reasoning-driven framework that shifts learning from out-come fitting to process modeling. REFORM adopts a three-stage curriculum that first in-duces forensic rationales, then aligns reasoning with final judgments, and finally refines logical consistency via reinforcement learning. To sup-port this paradigm, we introduce ROM , a large-scale dataset with rich reasoning annotations. Extensive experiments show that REFORM establishes new state-of-the-art performance with superior generalization, achieving 81.52% ACC on ROM, 76.65% ACC on DGM4, and 74.9 F1 on MMFakeBench. The code is avail-able at https://github.com/YcZhangSing/ REFORM .

While this technological renaissance empowers cre-ativity, it simultaneously lowers the barrier for fab-ricating hyper-realistic misinformation. The prolif-eration of sophisticated multimodal manipulations, ranging from subtle face swaps to fully synthesized news events, poses severe threats to information

result-oriented supervision , tends to encourage models to fit specific statistical artifacts rather than cultivating a deep, transferable understanding of the underlying forensic logic. As illustrated in Fig. 1 (a), relying solely on out-come supervision struggles to equip models with the intrinsic logic required for robust generaliza-tion. The essential reason is that optimizing exclu-sively for the final prediction (i.e., "Is this fake?") does not guarantee that the model implicitly learns the underlying forensic rationale, often leading to performance degradation when facing novel forg-eries (Chu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a). To address this, we argue that the core of generalizable detection lies in explicitly cultivating forensic rea-soning capabilities, as depicted in Fig.1 (b). Akin to a human forensic analyst, a model should not merely memorize the appearance of a forgery but rather deduce why an image-text pair is inconsis-tent through a logical chain of evidence. However, simply supervising this reasoning process is insuf-ficient. While Supervised Fine-Tuning establishes a basic alignment, it remains constrained by the passive imitation of static annotations (Chu et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025). Motivated by this insight, we propose anew framework that shifts the learning ob-jective from outcome fitting to Reasoning-Driven Optimization . To this end, we pro-pose Reasoning-Enhanced Forensic Optimization via Reinforcement Modeling (REFORM ), areasoning-endowed architecture designed to de-tect, ground, and explain multimodal manipula-tions. To effectively cultivate this capability, we implement a progressive three-stage learning cur-riculum. First, we perform Cognitive Reasoning Warm-up , teaching the model to articulate forensic rationales via data distillation. Second, we execute 

Reasoning-Endowed Joint Fine-Tuning , where the model learns to align its final judgment with its reasoning chain. Finally, recognizing that super-vised learning suffers from exposure bias and lacks the ability to self-correct, we introduce Constraint-Aware Policy Refinement . Leveraging Reinforce-ment Learning with Group Relative Policy Opti-mization (Shao et al., 2024b), we incentivize the model to explore optimal reasoning paths that are logically consistent with the final verdict. This opti-mization strictly constrains the generation process with forensic accuracy, enabling REFORM to in-ternalize robust judgment logic rather than fitting domain-specific patterns. To rigorously benchmark this new paradigm, we construct Reasoning-enhanced analysis for 

Omnibus Manipulation ( ROM ) dataset. Beyond the face-centric scope of previous benchmarks (Zhang et al., 2025c; Shao et al., 2023), ROM in-troduces scene-level synthesis and, crucially, pro-vides detailed reasoning annotations for over 704k 

samples to support process-oriented learning. In summary, our contributions are threefold: • We identify the limitations of result-oriented ma-nipulation detection and propose a paradigm shift towards reasoning-driven analysis, implementing REFORM, a new framework for robust multi-modal manipulation detection, grounding, and forensic explanation. • We introduce a progressive three-stage training framework culminating in a GRPO-based RL phase. This approach realizes Reasoning-Driven Optimization, effectively aligning the model’s cognitive process with forensic logic to distin-guish intrinsic anomalies from statistical biases. • We curate ROM, a large-scale, comprehensive benchmark that includes omnibus manipulation beyond face-related manipulations and provides high-quality reasoning supervision, setting a new standard for interpretable multimodal forensics.

Multimodal Misinformation Detection. Given challenging aligned forgeries (Shao et al., 2024a), recent approaches typically leverage external knowledge or refine internal features. For in-stance, FKA-Owl (Liu et al., 2024) utilizes LVLMs for factual verification, while RamDG (Shen et al., 2025) cross-references news with attribute databases. Conversely, feature-centric methods, 

e.g. , AMD (Zhang et al., 2025c), encode artifact tokens alongside semantic content. However, these methods predominantly rely on result-oriented su-pervision . This paradigm usually encourages short-cut learning (Zhang et al., 2025a), causing models to overfit statistical artifacts rather than logical ev-idence, which degrades generalization on unseen domains (Chu et al., 2025).

3 Methodology 

3.1 Data Preparation 

To support our process reasoning-focused train-ing as well as construct a comprehensive bench-mark for generalization evaluation, we prepare Reasoning-enhanced analysis for Omnibus Ma-nipulation (ROM) dataset, a full-scene large-scale benchmark with thinking annotation. While incor-porating face-centric samples from MDSM (Zhang et al., 2025c), ROM significantly transcends previ-ous boundaries by expanding the scope to scene-level synthesis and integrating logical reasoning. As illustrated in Fig. 2, the ROM dataset comprises 

704,456 image-text pairs across five news domains and nine manipulation categories. 

Manipulation Scope Expansion. ROM assimi-lates six face-specific classes from MDSM (Zhang et al., 2025c), comprising Original (Orig), FaceAt-tribute (FA), FaceSwap (FS), TextFabrication (TF), and their composites (FA&TF, FS&TF). Crucially,

FullGeneration (FG), and their text-fabricated vari-ants (BR&TF, FG&TF). This expansion moves beyond local facial edits to holistic scene syn-thesis, utilizing diverse generative models (e.g., SD3 (Esser et al., 2024), FLUX.1 (Labs et al., 2025)) to ensure artifact diversity. Reasoning Data Distillation. We augment ROM with rationale annotations to enhance interpretabil-ity. Using InternVL3.5-30B (Wang et al., 2025b), we generate textual reasoning for each image-text pair given its manipulation label. Fig. 2 visualizes these reasoning samples. As shown in Fig. 3, gen-erated rationales typically peak around 130 tokens, offering significantly richer context than standard short answers ( <20 tokens). Detailed prompts are in the Appendix G.

3.4 Reasoning-Endowed Joint Fine-Tuning 

Having established a coherent forensic logic foun-dation, we transition to the Supervised Fine-Tuning stage to endow the model’s judgment logic with these forensic reasoning capabilities. This phase is executed through two key strategies: 

Dual-Stream Generative Optimization. First, we activate the model’s full capacity for simultaneous reasoning and judgment. As shown in Fig. 4c, we unfreeze the entire backbone, including Em and 

Da. The model is now tasked with generating both the reasoning chain R and the structured answer 

A. Thus, we optimize both streams via language modeling losses LLM r and LLM a , where LLM a

mirrors Eq. 2 on answers annotations. 

Reason-Answer Semantic Alignment. Solely minimizing generative losses independent of each other could lead to logical discrepancies, where the generated reasoning contradicts the final verdict. To bridge this potential semantic gap, we intro-duce the Reason-Answer Consistency Loss (LRAC ). This objective enforces a minimum semantic sim-ilarity between the representation of the rationale and the answer, ensuring the reasoning trajectory effectively substantiates the final answer. We derive fixed-size global embeddings vR and 

vA via mean pooling over hidden states, masked to exclude padding tokens to capture only valid semantic content. We then employ a margin-based hinge loss to penalize alignments falling below a threshold η:

LRAC = max {0, η − cos( vR, vA)}. (3) Overall, the full objectives for this Reasoning-Endowed Joint Fine-Tuning stage is: 

LRJF = LLM r + LLM a + LRAC . (4) 

3.5 Constraint-Aware Policy Refinement 

To mitigate SFT’s exposure bias and encourage the exploration of optimal reasoning, we adopt Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) with a multi-dimensional reward function R consisting of four components: 

Consistency Reward ( Rc) measures the semantic alignment between the generated reasoning chain 

R and the predicted manipulation types, denoted as ˆci (image-modal) and ˆct (text-modal). We in-troduce a Consistency Verifier Vϕ to evaluate the logical entailment between the reasoning and these answer components. We instantiate Vϕ using a lightweight TinyBERT encoder (Jiao et al., 2020) equipped with two parallel classification heads for both modalities. Prior to RL training, Vϕ is pre-trained on ground-truth reason-label pairs, achiev-ing over 99% classification accuracy. This ensures 

Vϕ can reliably deduce the correct manipulation category solely from a reasoning description. The consistency reward is calculated by checking if 

Vϕ’s deductions match the predictions from Da:

Rc = I(V iϕ = ˆ ci) + I(V tϕ = ˆ ct), (5) where I(·) is the indicator function. 

Accuracy Reward ( Ra) aligns predictions with ground-truth labels via binary verification ( Rbin )and fine-grained type recognition ( Rf in ). Given ground truths ybin , c i, c t and predictions ˆy, ˆci, ˆct,5we formulate the reward as: 

Rbin = I(ˆ y = ybin ), (6) 

Rf in = I(ˆ ci = ci) + I(ˆ ct = ct), (7) 

Ra = Rbin + Rf in . (8) 

Grounding Reward ( Rg) evaluates the spatial pre-cision by calculating the Intersection over Union (IoU) between the predicted bounding boxes ˆb and the ground truth boxes b:

Rg = IoU (ˆb, b ). (9) 

Format Reward ( Rf ) enforces strict adherence to the specified output structure, as defined in Sec. 3.2. Let ˆa be the generated answer string and S be the set of valid regex patterns: 

Rf = I(ˆ a ∈ S ). (10)

5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we address the generalization chal-lenge by shifting from result-oriented supervision to explicit forensic reasoning. We propose RE-FORM, which integrates cognitive priming and a dual-decoder architecture, optimized via a GRPO-based curriculum to align judgment with logical ev-idence. Additionally, we contribute ROM, a large-scale benchmark expanding the scope to scene-level synthesis with reasoning annotations. Ex-periments demonstrate that REFORM significantly outperforms SOTA methods in cross-domain and zero-shot settings, establishing a robust paradigm for interpretable forensics. 86 Limitations 

Despite the superior performance of REFORM, it has several limitations that merit future study. 

Dependence on Distilled Rationales. Our train-ing relies on reasoning annotations distilled from larger MLLMs (InternVL). Although we employ Reinforcement Learning to refine the policy, the model’s upper bound is partially constrained by the quality and correctness of the teacher model’s forensic knowledge. Hallucinations in the teacher model could potentially introduce noise into the reasoning supervision. 

Inference Latency in Reasoning Mode. While our Fast Mode achieves high throughput suit-able for real-time screening, the Explainable Mode requires auto-regressive generation of rea-soning chains, which increases computational cost (1.03 pairs/sec). Future work could explore non-autoregressive generation or token pruning to ac-celerate the reasoning process.

A Human Performance on Multimodal Misinformation Detection 

To empirically assess the vulnerability of the gen-eral public to multimodal misinformation, we con-ducted a human evaluation study. The primary objective was to gauge human capability in dis-cerning news authenticity, thereby highlighting the limitations of manual verification. 

Study Design. We randomly sampled 117 image-text pairs from the ROM dataset for evaluation. As shown in Figure 5, the test set encompasses four distinct categories: authentic news (Orig), image-only manipulation (F-I), text-only manipulation (F-T), and joint manipulation of both image and text (F-I&T). Participants were required to identify the specific manipulation type for each sample. We recruited 18 volunteers, all holding a bachelor’s de-gree or higher, from our academic and professional networks. Informed consent was obtained from all participants prior to the study. They participated voluntarily without financial compensation. 

Fine-grained Classification Analysis. The chal-lenge inherent in this task is evident in the fine-grained classification results shown in Figure 6(a). While participants achieved a moderate accuracy of 57.98% on authentic samples (Orig), their perfor-mance on manipulated categories was significantly compromised: 14.41% for F-I, 22.00% for F-T, and 24.44% for F-I&T. Notably, no manipulated category exceeded a 25% recognition rate .To further investigate the patterns of human error, we computed a row-normalized confusion matrix (Figure 6b). The results reveal that participants ex-12 Figure 5: The user interface of the human evaluation study where each participant is given pairs of news images and caption and asked to determine whether they are manipulated or not. 

hibited the greatest difficulty with F-T (Fake Text) samples, with a staggering 47.56% being misclas-sified as completely authentic. This observation suggests that human evaluators tend to overlook subtle semantic inconsistencies in textual descrip-tions compared to visual anomalies. 

Binary Detection Capabilities. Considering real-world scenarios, humans often do not need to identify the exact manipulated modality; it is of-ten sufficient to detect the presence of any form of misinformation to avoid being misled. Therefore, we utilize two binary metrics to quantify this ability as shown in Figure 6c: • Overall Recall: Defined as the proportion of manipulated items (F-I, F-T, F-I&T) correctly identified as any type of fake. Even under this relaxed criterion, participants only achieved an Overall Recall of 57.9%. When breaking this down by category, F-I samples exhibited the highest detection rate at 64.5%, whereas F-T dropped to 52.4% and F-I&T to 56.7%. This performance gap (12.1%) between im-age and text manipulation further corroborates that humans are less sensitive to textual incon-sistencies than to visual traces. • False Alarm Rate: Crucially, this moderate detection rate came at a high cost: a False Alarm Rate of 42.0%. This high error rate on authentic content indicates that participants adopted a hyper-vigilant, "better safe than sorry" strategy specific to the testing environ-ment. This suggests that the observed detec-tion performance is likely an optimistic upper bound. In a natural, unguarded social me-dia context where users are typically more relaxed, their actual sensitivity to misinforma-tion would inevitably be significantly lower than the results presented here. The human evaluation results expose a critical vulnerability: human evaluators demonstrate gen-erally low sensitivity to multimodal misinforma-tion, particularly when textual fabrication is in-volved. The high error rate implies that relying solely on human judgment is insufficient to miti-gate the spread of fake news. Consequently, these findings strongly motivate the urgent need for re-searching and developing high-precision foren-sic methods, which can discern subtle inconsis-tencies that evade human scrutiny.

B.1 Baseline Settings 

We adapt six state-of-the-art multi-modal methods to the ROM setting for comparison. These methods encompass two conventional multi-modal manip-ulation detection models, two Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM) -based detection frame-works, one MLLM-based agentic manipulation de-tection framework, and one general multi-modal learning approach: • HAMMER (Shao et al., 2023) is a pioneering model for multi-modal manipulation detec-tion and grounding. It employs two unimodal encoders to extract visual and textual forgery features, which are then aligned through con-trastive learning. Following this, a multi-branch transformer architecture with two spe-cialized decoders is utilized for manipulation detection and grounding. • HAMMER++ (Shao et al., 2024a) is a more powerful model that builds upon HAMMER by integrating contrastive learning from both global and local perspectives to capture fine-grained inconsistencies. • FKA-Owl (Liu et al., 2024) is a detection model designed on MLLM, demonstrating outstanding cross-domain performance. Since FKA-Owl does not natively support fine-grained classification tasks, we fine-tuned it using the same prompts as those used for RE-FORM to enable comparable evaluation. • AMD (Zhang et al., 2025c) is a unified frame-work built upon MLLM designed for the MLLM-driven semantic-aligned DGM4 task. It introduces an Artifact Pre-perception En-coding module to capture manipulation traces into learnable artifact tokens and utilizes Manipulation-Oriented Reasoning to generate grounded detection results via a sequence-to-sequence format. • MMD-Agent (Liu et al., 2025) is a training-free, MLLM-based agentic framework. It de-composes the detection task into sequential sub-tasks: text-based fact-checking (retriev-ing external knowledge), visual manipulation analysis, and cross-modal consistency verifi-cation. To adapt this pipeline for the ground-ing task in ROM, we modified its visual analy-sis prompt to explicitly request bounding box coordinates for manipulated regions. 14 • ViLT (Kim et al., 2021) serves as the general multi-modal learning baseline. It is a repre-sentative single-stream method where cross-modal interaction layers operate on the con-catenation of image and text inputs. For adap-tation to the forgery detection task, we add classification and detection heads to the corre-sponding outputs of the model. 

B.2 Evaluation Metrics 

To comprehensively evaluate our proposed ROM, we follow the rigorous evaluation protocols and metrics outlined in (Shao et al., 2023) for all manip-ulation detection and grounding tasks. The detailed evaluation setup is organized as follows: • Binary Classification. Accuracy (ACC) is adopted as the evaluation metric to measure the correctness of real/fake news classification results. • Multi-Label Classification. For multi-label classification tasks, we employ the mean Av-erage Precision (mAP) , which measures the per-class average precision and then takes the arithmetic mean across all manipulation types. This macro-averaged mAP provides a compre-hensive evaluation of the model’s overall per-formance across different manipulation types. • Manipulated Image Bounding Box Ground-ing. To evaluate the precision of predicted manipulated bounding boxes, we calculate the 

mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) be-tween the ground-truth and predicted coordi-nates for all testing samples. This metric quan-tifies the spatial overlap between detected re-gions and actual manipulated areas, reflecting the localization accuracy of the model. • Manipulated Text Token Grounding. In the DGM4 benchmark, an additional task of manipulated text token grounding is included. For this task, Precision ( Ptok ) is used as the evaluation metric to measure the accuracy of identifying manipulated text tokens within in-put sequences. This standardized evaluation framework ensures a systematic and comparative assessment of ROM across diverse manipulation scenarios, aligning with both general detection tasks and benchmark-specific requirements.

C Comprehensive Evaluation and Generalization Analysis 

To rigorous evaluate the robustness and general-izability of our REFORM, we conduct compre-hensive experiments across three distinct evalu-ation protocols. Specifically, our evaluation en-compasses: (1) Cross-domain Performance , as-sessed using the multi-domain news benchmark MDSM (Zhang et al., 2025c); (2) Zero-shot Gen-eralization , tested on a wide array of unseen mis-information datasets, , as illustrated in Fig. 7, in-cluding news-domain benchmarks (DGM 4 (Shao et al., 2024a), MMFakeBench (Liu et al., 2025), MiRAGe (Huang et al., 2024)), non-news sce-narios (COSMOS (Shivangi et al., 2023), AI-GUARD (Zhang et al., 2025b)), and high-fidelity facial forgery datasets (DiffFace (Chen et al., 2024), DiFF (Cheng et al., 2024)); and (3) Robustness Against Missing Modalities , validating model sta-bility under modality-incomplete conditions.

C.3 Robustness Against Missing Modalities 

In real-world scenarios, multimodal data streams may be incomplete or corrupted. To evaluate RE-FORM’s reliability under such conditions, we con-ducted inference-time experiments where either vi-sual or textual input was masked. This test assesses whether the model can maintain effective detection capabilities using only partial information. We evaluate the model on the test set under three settings without retraining: • ROM (full multimodal): The standard set-ting with complete image-text pairs (I, T )

from test set of ROM. • Text-Only Inference: The visual input I is replaced by a zero-tensor image I∅ (represent-ing a black image), testing the model’s ability to reason solely based on textual semantics. • Image-Only Inference: The textual input T

is replaced by an empty sequence T∅, testing the model’s capacity to identify forensic traces solely from visual signals. As shown in Tab. 8, REFORM demonstrates exceptional reliability and generalizability in simu-lated real-world scenarios where data streams are incomplete. In the Image-Only setting, the model retains high performance with a negligible drop (∼7-8%), ensuring robust usability even without captions. In the Text-Only setting, a larger perfor-mance drop ( ∼15%) is observed. This decline is inevitable, as verifying visual authenticity solely through text is inherently challenging without di-rect forensic evidence. Remarkably, despite this severe lack of visual signals, REFORM still main-tains a commendable average accuracy of over 70% (e.g., 75.47% on NYT). This confirms that while performance naturally degrades in single-modal set-tings, REFORM effectively maximizes the utility of available information to ensure valid detection even in degraded environments.

E Details of Proposed ROM 

The ROM dataset is constructed by extending the manipulation categories of the MDSM (Zhang et al., 2025c). We utilize high-quality image-text pairs from VisualNews (Fuxiao et al., 2021), NYTimes800k (Tran et al., 2020), and Good-News (Biten et al., 2019) as our source reposi-tory. ROM assimilates six face-specific classes from MDSM, comprising Original (Orig), FaceAt-tribute (FA), FaceSwap (FS), TextFabrication (TF), and their composites (FA&TF, FS&TF). Crucially, we extend this taxonomy by introducing four scene-level categories: BackgroundReplacement (BR), 

FullGeneration (FG), and their text-fabricated vari-ants (BR&TF, FG&TF). Fig. 8a presents the comprehensive distribution of the ROM dataset. The concentric pie chart illus-trates the dataset’s composition. The inner chart de-lineates the data source proportions: 62% (412,433 samples) are derived from the existing MDSM dataset, while 38% (263,344 samples) are newly extended to incorporate scene-level manipulations. The outer ring specifically breaks down the distri-bution of this newly extended portion. To ensure class balance alongside the introduction of scene-level manipulations, we proportionally increased the number of REAL and TF samples. Specifi-cally, the extended data consists of 150,181 REAL, 19,109 TF, 31,809 BG, 14,763 BG&TF, 32,013 FG, and 15,781 FG&TF samples. In terms of media sources, the final ROM dataset encompasses five distinct news domains. Detailed statistics and their specific roles in our experimen-tal setup are summarized in Table 11. It is worth noting that the significant variance in data volume across these domains is inherent to the distribu-tion of the source data, where The New York Times 

(NYT) and The Guardian are predominant in pub-licly available repositories. Leveraging this distri-bution, we establish a rigorous cross-domain eval-uation protocol: the two largest domains serve as training sources, each split into training, validation, and testing sets following an 8:1:1 ratio. In con-trast, the smaller domains (e.g. USA Today , The Washington Post , and BBC News ) are reserved ex-clusively for testing, serving as unseen domains to evaluate zero-shot generalization capabilities. E.2 Background Replacement Variants. 

This category focuses on manipulating the semantic context by radically altering the environmental set-ting while preserving the foreground subject ( If g ). As illustrated in Fig. 8g, this results in a scenario where the actor is real, but the location is fabricated. We constructed a total of 31,809 BR samples and 14,763 BR&TF samples. To generate these samples, we first utilize RMBG-2 (Zheng et al., 2024) to accurately seg-ment and isolate foreground subjects from the orig-inal image. Subsequently, we task Qwen2.5-VL to “imagine” a reasonable but distinct background description that is compatible with the isolated fore-ground. Using this description as a prompt, we randomly employ either Stable Diffusion Inpaint-ing (StabilityAI, 2023) or FLUX Inpainting (Labs et al., 2025) to synthesize a new background behind the fixed subjects. This randomized selection strat-egy enhances the diversity of generation artifacts while ensuring seamless lighting and perspective consistency. Based on this synthesized imagery, we define two distinct manipulation categories: • BR (Visual Fake only): In this variant, we pair the modified image with the original cap-tion. This creates a subtle conflict where the visual evidence (the new background) no longer supports the factual location described in the text, serving as a test for visual forensic grounding. • BR&TF (Multimodal Fake): This variant represents a comprehensive forgery. We first extract the specific named entities (e.g., per-son names) from the original context. We then prompt Qwen2.5-VL to hallucinate a com-pletely new news event that aligns with the newly synthesized background. This results in a fabricated caption that is semantically co-herent with the visual scene but factually false, creating a highly deceptive multimodal news piece. 22 F Case Study

F.3 Limitations and Failure Cases 

While REFORM achieves state-of-the-art perfor-mance, analyzing its failure cases provides critical insights into the boundaries of reasoning-driven forensics. As shown in Fig. 13, we categorize rep-resentative failures into four dimensions aligned with the model’s cognitive process: 

Logical Hallucination. In Fig. 13a, the model correctly detects the text fabrication but justifies it with a logical fallacy. It argues that the subject could not perform the action in 2015 because she passed away in 2020 ( 2020 > 2015 ). This Ratio-nale Hallucination suggests that while REFORM learns the tone of forensic verification from the teacher model, it lacks an external knowledge base to validate the temporal logic of specific entities, a side-effect of the distillation process.

Sensory Limits in Low-Fidelity Imagery. Phys-ical image quality remains a hard bottleneck. Fig. 13c illustrates a False Negative case where a Face Swap is missed. Fig. 13d highlights the visual encoder’s struggle with Out-of-Distribution (OOD) data. Future Directions. Our failure analysis high-lights two critical avenues for further improvement: refining the quality of reasoning and exploring its deeper utility. First, regarding the logical hallucina-tions observed in Case (a), prior research indicates that student models usually benefit from more ro-bust knowledge distillation protocols. Strategies such as noise filtering or retrieval-augmented su-pervision usually mitigate teacher-induced halluci-nations and ensure high-fidelity student learning. Second, beyond generation, there is significant po-tential in better leveraging rationales to guide the detection process. Mechanisms such as rationale-guided attention usually alleviate semantic masking (Case b), while uncertainty-aware reasoning usu-ally modulates decision confidence effectively in challenging low-fidelity or OOD scenarios (Case c and d). H Statements 

H.1 Ethical Considerations 

This work adheres to the ACL Code of Ethics. The ROM dataset and associated analyses were created solely to support research on detecting and grounding multimodal manipulations. We recognize that assembling realistic synthetic ex-amples entails dual-use risks: the same materials and procedures could be misused to produce de-ceptive content. To minimize harm, we adopt a harm-minimizing, controlled-release approach: we will not publish the generation pipeline, detailed prompts, or prompt–response pairs to prevent their exploitation by adversaries for generating harm-ful content; public distribution is limited to vetted, research-only access under a signed Data Usage Agreement (DUA); distributed images will carry conspicuous visual watermarks and standardized metadata tags; high-fidelity originals and sensi-tive metadata will be withheld; images of minors and clearly sensitive contemporary conflict content have been excluded; and we reserve the right to revoke access on evidence of misuse. Full techni-cal and procedural details of these safeguards are documented in the dataset README file. 

H.2 Data Privacy and Safety 

We prioritize the privacy and safety of individuals. The raw images and captions in the ROM dataset are derived exclusively from established, publicly available academic datasets (NYTimes800k, Good-News, and VisualNews), which have been widely adopted in top-tier conferences and have under-gone prior ethical scrutiny. These source datasets consist of published news media and typically do not contain sensitive personally identifiable infor-mation (PII) of private citizens. In our curation process, we adhered to strict protocols to ensure that no private PII is exposed. Furthermore, regard-ing offensive content, as detailed in Section H.1, we implemented additional filtering steps to ex-clude images involving minors, graphic violence, or clearly sensitive contemporary conflict content. We conducted manual verification on random sub-sets to ensure compliance with these standards. 

H.3 Reproducibility Statement 

To ensure the reproducibility of our research, we provide comprehensive code, data, and experimen-tal details. (1) Code : The source code for all mod-els, along with scripts to run experiments and ana-lyze results, will be available at GitHub. The repos-itory will include a detailed README.md file with instructions for installing dependencies and repro-ducing our findings.
