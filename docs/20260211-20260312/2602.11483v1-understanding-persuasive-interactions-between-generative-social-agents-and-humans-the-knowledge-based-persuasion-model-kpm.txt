Abstract 

Generative social agents (GSAs) use artificial intelligence to autonomously com-municate with human users in a natural and adaptive manner. Currently, there is a lack of theorizing regarding interactions with GSAs, and likewise, few guidelines exist for studying how they influence user attitudes and behaviors. Consequently, we propose the Knowledge-based Persuasion Model (KPM) as a novel theoreti-cal framework. According to the KPM, a GSA’s self-, user-, and context-related knowledge drives its persuasive behavior, which in turn shapes attitudes and behaviors of a responding human user. By synthesizing existing research, the model offers a structured approach to studying interactions with GSAs, support-ing the development of agents that motivate rather than manipulate humans. Accordingly, the KPM encourages the integration of responsible GSAs that adhere to social norms and ethical standards with the goal of increasing user wellbeing. Implications of the KPM for research and application domains such as healthcare and education are discussed.

# 1 Introduction 

What took life millions of years of evolution, has been mirrored by artificial intelligence in a remarkably short time: Generative social agents (GSAs) have become able to communicate in a highly natural, humanlike, and adaptive manner [1, 2]. We use them to seek guidance and information [3–5], as creative partners for co-writing and ideation [6, 7], or simply for companionship and social support [5, 8]. While doing so, they can take on various forms such as generative chatbots [4, 9, 10], avatars [11–13], videogame characters [14–16], or social robots [17–21]. Unlike earlier generations of conversational agents, GSAs are able to complete complex social tasks more seamlessly, such as providing consulting services [22, 23] or motivational coaching [24–26]. As a result, they may take on roles that come with social responsibility, for instance, by serving as teaching aids [27, 28] or healthcare assistants [29–31]. Simultaneously, GSAs have become highly persuasive [32], in some instances even more so than humans [33, 34]. On the one hand, this provides promising opportunities for GSAs to improve human wellbeing, for example by motivating users to adopt healthier or more productive lifestyles [35]. On the other hand, GSAs also raise concerns regarding the potential misuse of their social influence, such as manipulating users into irresponsible actions or disseminating false information [31, 36, 37]. These opportunities and dangers highlight the need to deepen our understanding of persuasive interactions between GSAs and humans, thus ensuring that GSAs act responsibly, complying with human-centered values, ethical standards and prevailing social norms. Persuasion is the process of creating or modifying emotions, cognitions or behav-iors [38]. It represents an integral part of communication and social interactions in humans [39] and agents alike [34, 40]. Currently, there is a lack of theorizing regard-ing persuasive interactions between human users and GSAs. Previous research mostly investigated human responses to predefined persuasive agent messages, rather than autonomously generated agent behavior [40, 41]. A prominent example in human-robot interaction research is the Wizard-of-Oz approach, in which a robot is remotely con-trolled by a human operator for experimental purposes [42]. While rule-based methods such as Wizard-of-Oz are valuable for examining human responses to controlled, prede-fined persuasion attempts, they are not well suited to investigate persuasive behaviors that are autonomously generated by the agents themselves [43, 44]. As GSAs mirror human behavior by learning from vast amounts of human-generated data [45], it is timely to investigate the informational prerequisites that drive agentic behavior from a psychological perspective. In the present work, we define agent knowledge as the structured set of informa-tion that a GSA has internalized and that is used as a stimulus to generate behavioral responses. This notion is inspired by knowledge-based systems , which refers to explicit, 2symbolic knowledge representations (i.e., rules, facts, ontologies) combined with infer-ence mechanisms to guide problem-solving [46]. While rooted in this tradition, GSAs extend the notion of knowledge to include not only explicit inputs such as prompts (i.e., situated knowledge ), but also implicit, distributed patterns acquired during train-ing (i.e., embedded knowledge ). Importantly, the behavior generation of GSAs is based on stochastic processes, meaning that model outputs are non-deterministic and cannot be fully anticipated based on computational means [45, 47]. However, to guide agentic behavior, an agent’s embedded knowledge can be modified through methods such as data curation or fine-tuning, and situated knowledge can be adjusted with techniques like prompt-engineering or retrieval-augmented generation [48–50]. This highlights the need to identify knowledge-based design requirements that lead to responsible agent behaviors [51, 52]. Consequently, we need to understand not merely how human users respond to predefined agent behaviors, but also how agentic persuasion emerges based on available knowledge, marking a paradigm shift from researching rule-based agents (Figure 1).

Following this approach, the current work introduces the Knowledge-based Per-suasion Model (KPM) as a theoretical framework for understanding persuasive interactions with GSAs. The aim of the KPM is to investigate the relationships between the three dimensions agent knowledge, agent persuasive behavior, and human response . More specifically, the model aims to understand how an agent’s avail-able knowledge impacts its persuasive behavior, and how this behavior is processed by human users. By identifying patterns of agent knowledge that lead to desirable outcomes , we may strategically modify an agent’s access to information to foster per-suasion that aligns with user goals and values. As such, the KPM can be used to inform the responsible development and integration of persuasive GSAs in applied contexts such as healthcare and education [51, 52]. To this end, the KPM synthesizes and extends existing theories and research from psychology, human-agent interactions, and information systems, providing a comprehensive framework for our understanding of persuasive GSAs. 

# 2 Theoretical Foundations 

Psychological theories have often been used as a theoretical foundation in human-agent interaction research to explain how human users interact with persuasive technologies [40, 41]. The Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM) [53] and the Heuristic-Systematic Model of information processing (HSM) [54] represent the most popular psychological 3approaches that have been applied to model how users process persuasive cues and form attitudes in interactions with conversational agents. The ELM posits two primary routes of persuasion: the central route and the peripheral route. The central route involves deep, thoughtful consideration of the content and arguments presented in a message. Persuasion follows the central route, particularly when people are motivated and able to engage in deep elaboration. The peripheral route, on the other hand, relies on superficial cues rather than the message content. For instance, cues like the attractiveness of the speaker, the number of arguments presented, or the presence of consensus cues may impact persuasion. Information processing follows the peripheral route when the audience lacks motivation or ability to process the message deeply. Persuasion through the peripheral route tends to be more temporary and susceptible to change. [53] Relatedly, the HSM distinguishes between systematic processing and heuristic pro-cessing. Systematic processing involves comprehensive and analytical evaluation of message content and, similar to the central route in the ELM, requires higher levels of motivation and cognitive effort. Heuristic processing, on the other hand, relies on mental shortcuts to evaluate a message. Common heuristics include credibility (e.g., “Experts can be trusted.”) and consensus (e.g., “If others agree, it must be good.”). Like the peripheral route in the ELM, heuristic processing is more likely under condi-tions of low motivation or limited capacity, and the resulting attitudes are generally more prone to change. Importantly, in contrast to the ELM, the HSM argues that heuristic and systematic processing can also occur simultaneously. [54] Apart from dual process models of persuasion like ELM and HSM, the Unimodel [55] proposes that persuasion proceeds via a single inferential route, with both message arguments and peripheral cues regarded as functionally equivalent impact factors. As such, they are integrated within the same reasoning process. and persuasion emerges from the audience’s motivation and ability to evaluate them rather than from qual-itatively distinct processing routes. However, while the ELM, HSM, and Unimodel offer explanations on how human receivers process persuasive messages, they typically include messages sent by human agents and are not specifically tailored to the context of persuasive GSAs [56]. A framework that may explain how human users come to accept emerging tech-nologies like GSAs is the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) [57]. It suggests that two primary factors, perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness , impact an individ-ual 's attitude towards technology and, consequently, their intention to use it. Perceived ease of use refers to the degree to which a person believes that using a technology will be free of effort, while perceived usefulness is the degree to which a person believes that a technology will enhance their performance or personal efficiency. These percep-tions lead to the formation of behavioral intentions, which ultimately predict actual technology usage [57]. In the past, the TAM model has been extended to fit the con-text of persuasive technologies. For example, the persuasive technology acceptance model, arguing that an agent’s credibility , ease of use , and usefulness impact per-suasiveness [58]. Further, Ghazali et al. [59] based their Persuasive Robot Acceptance Model (PRAM) on the TAM by taking into account social responses such as trust and liking as predictors of compliance and reactance in agent driven persuasion. 4The ELM, HSM, Unimodel, and TAM have in common that they primarily focus on how persuasive attempts are processed by the human receiver of an agent’s persuasive message. In HAI research, those theories imply that when humans respond to the persuasive behavior of a social agent, both their attitude towards the agent’s persuasive behavior (e.g., attitude towards the content of the agent’s message) and the attitude towards the agent itself (e.g., trust, liking, acceptance) influence the effectiveness of the persuasion attempt. However, their strong focus on the receiver of the persuasive message, rather than the sender of the persuasive message, might not be suitable to answer the question of how GSAs persuade human users based on the knowledge available to them. A framework that explains persuasion in human-human interactions from the view-point of both agent and receiver of persuasive messages, is the Persuasion Knowledge Model (PKM) [60][]. Its focus lies on understanding how individuals cope with and respond to persuasion attempts by considering the knowledge agents and receivers have about persuasion tactics and strategies (Figure 2). The PKM includes three main components: persuasion knowledge , target/agent knowledge , and topic knowledge . Tar-get knowledge entails the information an agent has about the receiver of a persuasive message, such as the demographics or political views of the interaction partner. Topic knowledge refers to the information regarding the persuasion domain about which the agent wants to change the receiver’s mind, such as the persuasion topic fitness , if the agent tries to persuade the receiver to conduct a fitness exercise. Lastly, persuasion knowledge , which is the main focus of the PKM, refers to knowledge about the process of persuasion itself, such as knowledge about persuasive strategies. However, the PKM was developed with human agents in mind and doesn’t account for attributes that are unique to GSAs including potential advantages like their ability to adapt their own personality or to retrieve large amounts of information, as well as potential disadvan-tages like limited sensory capabilities and motor control in relation to humans. Hence it does not include knowledge that might be oblivious and immutable to humans, such as a GSA’s knowledge about its own role and personality, or information about the physical environment.

Taken together, no currently available theoretical framework is fully suitable to understand persuasive interactions with GSAs. The TAM, PRAM, ELM, HSM, and Unimodel explain how human receivers process a persuasive message, but they fail to consider the perspective of how the persuasive behavior of an agent is impacted by its available knowledge. On the other hand, the PKM encompasses a dual perspective 5that includes both agents and targets of persuasion, but is strongly focused on human, rather than agent knowledge. In addition, the PKM does not specify which aspects of persuasive agent behavior, and user attitudes towards the agent impact persuasion effectiveness. To fill these gaps, the KPM combines and extends key aspects of these theories and translates them to the context of persuasive GSAs. The ELM, HSM, TAM, Unimodel, and PRAM framework help to understand how human users respond to an agent’s persuasion attempt. Furthermore, from the PKM, the KPM integrates the perspective of the sender of a persuasive message, as well as the focus on how knowledge impacts persuasion. The KPM further extends these types of knowledge and tailors them to the context of artificial agents. In addition, the KPM considers both how human users respond to an agent’s persuasion attempt, and what behavior the agent executes based on the knowledge it has access to. 

# 3 Towards the Knowledge-based Persuasion Model 

The KPM is a theoretical process model which serves to understand persuasive inter-actions between GSAs and humans. It features three core dimensions that follow a sequential structure: agent knowledge impacts agent persuasive behavior , which in turn impacts user response .

## 3.1 Agent Knowledge 

Agent Knowledge is the first dimension of the KPM framework. To increase our under-standing in persuasive human-agent interactions, it is essential to investigate which types of knowledge impact a GSA’s persuasive behavior. To this end, three subcate-gories of agent knowledge have been identified : self-knowledge , user-knowledge , and 

context-knowledge [21, 52]. 

Self-knowledge refers to the information a GSA has accumulated about itself. This includes the agent’s role and personality profile it is supposed to assume, including information about the agent’s own capabilities and expected behaviors. We adapted this construct from generative AI literature, where it is defined as information available to an LLM about its own functionalities and characteristics [49, 61]. Compared to humans that naturally develop a stable sense of identity through lived experience, AI systems can be prompted with roles or personalities to achieve similar coherence. Research shows that generative agents can adopt different personality profiles through prompting, fine-tuning, or data curation [62–69] and that their persuasiveness varies depending on the adopted profile [70]. Possible indicators include Big-5 personality traits [62, 63, 65, 66, 68, 71, 72], dark triad personality traits [65], or specific character profiles [67]. Assertiveness – the degree to which an agent claims its will, while at the same time adhering to polite social etiquette [73] – represents a trait particularly relevant to persuasion [74–76]. However, unlike humans who commonly develop a stable identity, LLM personalities are less consistent and may shift during interactions [63]. This dynamic raises concerns in persuasive contexts, where sudden changes of the agent’s character could be perceived as manipulative. Ensuring consistent self-knowledge is therefore essential for responsible and effective agent behavior [69]. 6User-knowledge refers to the accumulated information a GSA has about its human interaction partner, a concept adapted from target knowledge in the PKM [60]. User-knowledge enables personalized persuasion by allowing the agent to tailor its messages and behavior to individual users. It encompasses both prior knowledge available before the interaction and information learned during the conversation, for exam-ple through user inputs, or by retrieving information from the internet or databases. Research on LLMs indicates that personalization significantly increases persuasive-ness: for example, messages adapted to user personality traits, political ideology, or moral foundations exert stronger influence than non-personalized messages [77]. In educational settings, generative agents aware of learner knowledge, preferences, and interests, have been shown to induce higher motivation and academic performance in students [78, 79]. Similarly, LLMs that incorporated user preferences in domains such as movie recommendations [80] or text editing [81] were perceived as more persuasive. Information about emotional user cues – whether vocal [82, 83] or visual [84] – may further invoke empathetic behavior that invokes greater user trust and acceptance, provided the GSA has the capability to detect these emotions. 

Context-knowledge encompasses all available information about situational circum-stances of a persuasive interaction, other than information regarding the agent or the user. A GSA’s access to such information is crucial to enable context-sensitive interac-tions. One important aspect of context-knowledge is a GSAs domain-specific expertise, or in other words, its topic knowledge [60]. This is especially important to ensure truthful and competent agent behaviors that foster user trust. In this regard, deeper domain expertise in fine-tuned LLMs has been associated with better performances in debates by delivering more informed and convincing arguments [85]. In addition, per-suasion knowledge [60] is important to guide the agent towards the use of adequate persuasive strategies, for example to foster intrinsic learning motivation in students [51]. Furthermore, contextual factors like information about the agent’s application context or physical environment may be important for motivational purposes. For example, an agent in eldercare settings might use information about upcoming events that take place in the nursing home to promote therapy attendance [52]. The KPM posits that a GSA’s knowledge shapes its behavior. Accordingly, we hypothesize that the availability of self-knowledge , user-knowledge , and context-knowledge impacts the agent’s persuasive behavior. Hence, by experimentally varying an agent’s knowledge, we can investigate which informational prerequisites impact the agent’s behavior in ways that enhance or diminish persuasion effectiveness. Follow-ing this approach, the KPM allows us to explore how different knowledge variables contribute to desirable or unwarranted agent behaviors. For instance, the KPM could help identify the optimal personality of a tutoring GSA, by only including aspects of 

self-knowledge that lead to responsible and motivating pedagogical behaviors. How-ever, to explore the effects of an agent’s knowledge on the agent’s persuasive behavior, it is crucial to determine relevant expressions of such behaviors. 73.2 Agent Persuasive Behavior 

The second dimension of the KPM, agent persuasive behavior, refers to autonomously generated communicative behavior by GSAs. We suggest two categories of agent per-suasive behavior based on psychological theories of information processing [53–55]: 

Message characteristics and message delivery. Message characteristics focus on the content of the agent’s persuasive communication – essentially, what the agent says to influence the human user. In contrast, message delivery emphasizes the presentation of this content, covering both verbal and nonverbal elements that shape how the agent conveys its message. Concerning message characteristics , the number of arguments and the length of the message [53, 54], as well as the quality of arguments [86–90] have been shown to impact persuasion. For example, including the desirable outcome of a message and a clear description of the consequences of following the messages has led to increased argument quality [91]. Further, LLM-generated messages that use expert-verified information indicate higher credibility and persuasiveness [9]. Other relevant message character-istics include the application of persuasive strategies. Examples of such persuasive strategies are disrupt-then-reframe [92] or the foot-in-the-door technique [93]. Disrupt-then-reframe implies using unexpected phrasings to disrupt a person’s understanding of a situation to facilitate a new representation of that situation. The foot-in-the-door technique seeks to reach agreement to a large request by first securing compliance with a smaller, more modest request. It is important to note though, that variations of single message design choices have relatively small effect sizes on increasing message persuasiveness [94], which is why multiple features of the message should be considered when rating the agent’s persuasive behavior. Regarding message delivery , multiple studies in persuasive HRI featuring non-GSAs have shown that the way an agent delivers its message influences the agent’s persuasion effectiveness [74, 95–100]. Generally, the better an agent can express itself, the more persuasive the agent is perceived as [40]. For example, human-robot inter-action research has demonstrated that the speed and pitch of the voice [95], the vocal tone [74], gazing [99, 100], head movements [101], gestures [71, 99, 102], and facial emotions [97, 98, 103] increase agent persuasiveness. In addition, social feedback like smiling when doing something well or frowning when doing something wrong has been shown to increase persuasiveness of social robots [98, 104]. We assume that the GSA 's self-knowledge will enable it to tailor its arguments and delivery style based on its role and personality, u ser-knowledge will help the agent align the message to the recipient’s preferences and understanding, and context-knowledge 

will allow the agent to adapt the persuasive behavior based on the situation. There-fore, the agent’s ability to generate persuasive messages will depend on the integration of these knowledge types, influencing both the structure of the message and the per-suasive strategies used. As such, the KPM views agent persuasive behavior as the link between agent knowledge and user response. While the agent’s behavior is depen-dent on its self-knowledge , user-knowledge , and context-knowledge, user responses are shaped by individual interpretations of these behaviors. Hence, in order to under-stand persuasive interactions between humans and GSAs, we also need to investigate 8how human users are impacted by an agents persuasion attempt, and which factors ultimately lead to user compliance or reactance. 

## 3.3 User Response 

User response is the third dimension of the KPM. It refers to how human interaction partners process the GSA’s persuasive behavior. This ultimately determines persua-sion effectiveness, which is defined as a change in t opic affect (e.g., liking of fitness if the persuasion topic was fitness), topic cognition (e.g., knowledge gains about fitness), and / or behavior (e.g., complying to do a fitness task). Psychological theories like the ELM [53], HSM [54] and the Unimodel [55] indicate that persuasion effectiveness is influenced both by how the persuasive message and the persuasive agent are evaluated by human users. Similarly, the TAM [57] and the 

PRAM [59] posit that including attitudes and social responses towards the agent’s behavior are useful to predict agent persuasiveness. Consequently, attitudes towards message and attitudes towards agent form the basis of persuasion effectiveness in the KPM. Attitudes towards message , contains user evaluations of the agents message, as deemed relevant in psychological information processing theories like the ELM [53], HSM [54], Unimodel [55], and general psycho-logical communication research [90]. Attitudes towards agent includes variables like 

trust , liking, perceived usefulness , and usability of the agent, as deemed relevant for persuasion in the PRAM [59] and TAM [57] frameworks. 

## 3.4 Context 

Persuasive human-agent interactions are significantly influenced by the context in which they occur [40]. An important aspect of the interaction’s context involves user attributes that capture interindividual differences in user responses. Variables of the human user such as cultural background [105], familiarity with the agent [106], occupa-tion [107], the attitude towards agents in general [108], or personal tendencies towards conformity [109] can all shape how individuals perceive and respond to an agent’s persuasion attempts. Variations in the user’s motivation might also explain potential differences in processing persuasive messages [53, 54]. Furthermore, it is important to consider topic expertise [60]. Depending on the subject matter, the agent could pos-sess greater domain-specific knowledge than the user, or vice versa, affecting the flow and impact of the interaction. Relatedly, agent attributes such as embodiment [110, 111] and appearance [97, 112, 113] may impact user perception of the agent and, consequently, their receptiveness to its persuasive efforts. Hence, agents that evoke greater trust or relatability through their appearance may be more persuasive [59]. Additionally, social power dynamics between the agent and the user can shape persuasion, with factors such as the agent’s authority [114–116]. The agent’s functionalities are another vital consideration. For example, an agent capable of speech may be perceived as more useful than a purely text-based agent [110]. Similarly, an agent capable of expressive gestures, such as detailed arm and hand movements, may be more persuasive than a more static agent [99, 102]. Furthermore, 9the generative AI models used by the GSAs may largely impact the agents embedded knowledge , and how situated knowledge is processed, leading to potentially different outcomes [117, 118]. Hence, the agent’s system architecture and the specific generative AI models used should be well documented when conducting studies with GSAs. The nature of the task that a human user is persuaded to comply with is another critical contextual factor. Research indicates that task attributes like difficulty [102, 119, 120], type [121], and associated rewards [122, 123] determine how effectively an agent persuades. Tasks that align with the agent’s perceived expertise or capa-bilities tend to result in greater acceptance [23]. Lastly, the research environment in which the interaction takes place contributes to the persuasion process. Variables such as room temperature [124], the presence of other humans [125] or agents [126], and the experimental setting (lab vs. field) [127] can affect user responses. The research environment is particularly important if the agent can perceive or manipulate its sur-roundings, e.g., by changing the room temperature. Overall, multiple context factors must be considered when designing effective persuasive interactions with GSAs. To account for such factors, we add the additional layer of context to the KPM, which includes agent attributes , task attributes , user attributes , and research environment .

## 3.5 The Knowledge-based Persuasion Model (KPM) 

Through the dimensions of agent knowledge , agent persuasive behavior , user response, 

as well as the context layer, the KPM (Figure 3) enriches our understanding of inter-actions with persuasive GSAs such as generative chatbots, virtual agents, or social robots. The model explains how an agent’s self -, user -, and context -knowledge impact an agent’s behavior during a persuasion attempt. While the model typically focuses on a single persuasion attempt, it also allows for the examination of dynamics across mul-tiple persuasion attempts. For instance, researchers can use the KPM to analyze user compliance between multiple persuasive scenarios over the course of the interaction, by comparing data from different time points.  

> Fig. 3 The Knowledge-based Persuasion Model (KPM) for persuasive interactions between GSAs and humans

The KPM has four important prerequisites. First, in order to experimentally manipulate agent knowledge configurations, an agent’s knowledge must be adjustable, 10 e.g., through system prompting [50], retrieval augmented generation [128], or fine-tuning [48]. Second, the agent’s persuasive behavior must be observable, such as the agent delivering persuasive verbal or non-verbal messages. This implies that interaction transcripts for human-agent communication should be available to identify relevant message characteristics (e.g., use of persuasion strategies) and deliveries (e.g., use of non-verbal expressions). Third, user responses should be measurable. This includes the measurement of user attitudes through established scales, and the measurement of persuasion effectiveness, for example (e.g., number of compliances, or a change in resource allocation [21]). Fourth, the context of the interaction should be clearly defined and controlled for. Agent attributes and the research environment should be documented and held constant across conditions. Impacts of tasks attributes should be considered as well, especially if interactions entail multiple persuasive tasks. Lastly, user attributes should be accounted for by collecting data on potential confounders. The KPM is a conceptual model and relevant operationalizations of agent knowl-edge , agent behavior, and user response, as well as specific relationships between these variables have yet to be identified through empirical interaction studies. How-ever, based on existing research, preliminary indicators of agent knowledge (Section 3.2), agent persuasive behavior (Section 3.3), user response (Section 3.4), and context 

(Section 3.5) have been identified to help guide research (Table 1).

11 The core contribution of the KPM lies in specifying the mechanisms of persuasive interactions between GSAs and humans. It integrates existing theoretical models and expands them to the context of persuasive interactions with GSAs. The KPM also marks a directional change in approaching human-agent interaction research. It shifts the focus from predefined agent behaviors to informational perquisite that impact autonomously generated behaviors. Within the KPM, predications and impacts of these behaviors are based on empirical validation, rather than computational means. Hence, the KPM views agent information processing not merely from a system-based, but also from a psychological perspective. From a practical standpoint, the KPM can be used to guide GSA behavior. By iden-tifying the information an agent requires to responsibly motivate users for their own benefit, knowledge-based design requirements can be integrated into an agent’s system to foster responsible interactions. Specifically, using the KPM framework, knowledge-based design requirements for persuasive GSAs in eldercare [52] and higher education [51] have been identified. In education, for instance, the KPM can inform the devel-opment of robotic teaching aids tailored to inspire and support students. Such agents can utilize knowledge about individual learners and subject matter to deliver person-alized assistance and precise information. In healthcare, the KPM enables the design of socially assistive robots that encourage therapy compliance or healthy lifestyles. We assume that the GSA’s required knowledge will differ significantly across domains, as different types of agents engage in distinct tasks and with diverse types of users. This is why the context layer of the KPM should always be accounted for, especially when using the model for applied research. The KPM also facilitates the exploration of risks associated with GSAs. The unpre-dictability of black-box generative AI technologies can lead to negative consequences, such as deception [31, 36] or unsafe recommendations [37]. Awareness of these risks can help establish boundaries for the ethical deployment of persuasive GSAs, ensur-ing their use aligns with users’ best interests and prevents exploitative practices. In addition, safeguards may have to be considered to protect users exposed to potential irresponsible agent behavior and to allow human collaborators to intervene when nec-essary. Data protection is equally important, especially when GSAs rely on sensitive user information. Ensuring informed consent and using secure, anonymized data stor-age protocols is essential, especially when user-specific knowledge informs the agent’s behavior. Adhering to societal norms and ethical standards is even more essential when GSAs engage with vulnerable groups of users such as patients [139]. While the KPM introduces new opportunities to understand persuasive interac-tions with GSAs, two key challenges need to be addressed. First, GSAs use generative AI systems capable of producing highly unrestricted, context-sensitive outputs. Unlike agents with predefined actions, this variability introduces difficulties in maintaining consistency and comparability across participants. Hence, instead of varying or pre-defining an agent’s behavior, there is a need to measure it. This can be done by using objective measures (e.g., number and type of gestures used by the agent, length of agent messages) as well as qualitative evaluations (e.g., type of persuasion strat-egy applied). Alternatively, if impacts of agent knowledge on user responses are the 12 focus of the investigation, a qualitative manipulation check may be applied to investi-gate whether differences in agent knowledge configurations actually lead to observable differences in agent behaviors. Second, the scope of the KPM must be considered. The KPM was developed with a focus on understanding persuasive GSAs, rather than human-driven persuasion, which has already been extensively studied through established theories such as Cialdini’s seven principles [140]. Though incorporating reciprocal interactions (i.e., the human user persuading the agent) and multi-user dynamics (e.g., in debates) could broaden the scope of the KPM, this expansion would also add complexity and asymmetry, as human persuasion differs from agentic behavior generation. Nevertheless, future extensions of the KPM could integrate these dimensions to extend the KPM to additional contexts. To overcome these challenges, researchers should adopt best practices tailored to the unique capabilities and risks of GSAs. Mixed-method approaches, combin-ing qualitative insights from conversation transcripts with quantitative analyses such as structural equation modeling, are recommended for capturing the nuanced rela-tionships between variables of agent knowledge , agent persuasive behavior , and user response . Additionally, prioritizing realistic study designs, ethical considerations, and robust data protection protocols will help ensure that research with GSAs contributes meaningfully to both theoretical understanding and practical applications. When upholding such methodological standards, the KPM can provide a comprehensive framework for studying persuasive interactions with GSAs across diverse real-world contexts. 

# 4 Conclusion 

As technology continues to rapidly advance, a stronger understanding of persuasive human–agent interactions will be vital for developing GSAs that engage responsibly with human users. As autonomous agents become increasingly persuasive, restricting their access to certain types of knowledge may help prevent malicious or manipula-tive influence. At the same time, GSAs deployed for beneficial purposes – such as motivating users to adopt healthier lifestyles – may require access to knowledge that enables personalized, and context-sensitive persuasion. Therefore, studying persua-sive interactions with GSAs is essential to support human wellbeing while preserving autonomy and informed choice. The KPM provides a framework to study such interac-tions, ultimately promoting responsible persuasion in alignment with human interest while reducing the risk of manipulation and harm.
